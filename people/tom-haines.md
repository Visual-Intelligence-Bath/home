Tom obtained his PhD at York, working on 3D reconstruction using both stereopsis and shape from shading, before working on identifying abnormal behaviour in video at Queen Mary. From there he moved to UCL and working on generative models of handwriting and texture. At Bath he has been involved in projects that include detecting the fluorescent pattern of drugs, optimising experimental design in the search for novel chemicals/materials/processes, reasoning about noisy data from scientific instruments, and detecting underwater munitions. He also runs the CS departments GPU cluster and 3Dami, an educational non-profit that makes 3D animated films with pre-university students.

Tom specialises in rigorous Bayesian machine learning techniques that report their uncertainty and can explain themselves, plus non-parametric methods that adapt the complexity of the learned model depending on the quantity and complexity of the available data. These approaches are complemented with practical expertise in areas such as active learning and Bayesian optimisation, which lead to a specific interest in machine learning for science. Tom also works on machine learning operations from the point of view of high performance computing.
