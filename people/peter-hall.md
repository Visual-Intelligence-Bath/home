Humans can see or perceive objects in a vastly wider range of circumstances, in particular both natural wages artwork, and includes pareidolia, which suggests human recognition has a strong generative component. No computational system can yet match such performance. For 25 years, Peter's work has explored questions regarding both the analysis (recognition) and synthesis (generation) of artwork by computer. Peter's contributions to the field include: the first work to synthesise highly abstract art (akin to Picasso, Braque,  Matisse, Miro, child art, etc.); the first work to edit video for cartoon motion effects; the first work to paint strokes onto video in a stable way; the first work to analyse video to create models of 3D moving trees and water for artistic use; the first work to build a system for recognition over photos and artwork; among the first works to conduct style transfer using paint strokes; the first work to synthesise tactile images from photos, for use by people with visual impairment.

Peter regularly works with the Creative Sector, as well as colleagues from Arts and Humanities. He has set up a not-for-profit company (Pic2Tac) to distribute the software to produce tactile images. His interest in acting on perception and perceiving to act remains strong.
